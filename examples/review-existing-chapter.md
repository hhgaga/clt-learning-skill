# Example — REVIEW: existing textbook chapter

**User prompt:** *"Review this chapter on shoulder kinematics for teaching effectiveness."* (Imagine a 12-page chapter with one large multi-part figure on page 1 and dense prose pages 2–12.)

**Skill output:**

---

**Review pass against CLT effects.** Severity rated by impact on schema construction.

### High severity

1. **Split attention (high)** — Figure 1 (glenohumeral joint with 8 labeled muscle attachments) is on page 1; the prose explanation of each attachment is on pages 3–7. Reader must mentally hold the figure while reading 4 pages later, or flip back constantly. Both consume working memory that should be going to schema construction.
   - **Fix:** Inline each attachment description directly beside its label on the figure, or split the figure into 8 small panels each captioned in place.

2. **Redundancy (high)** — Pages 4 and 9 narrate the same "scapulohumeral rhythm 2:1 ratio" idea in prose, while Figure 3 already shows it graphically with arrows and angle labels. The prose adds no new information.
   - **Fix:** Delete the prose redescriptions. Keep one short caption: *"For every 3° of arm elevation, ~2° comes from the glenohumeral joint and ~1° from scapular upward rotation."*

3. **No worked examples (high)** — chapter is 100% expository. A novice reading about "force couples stabilizing the scapula" has no concrete instance to anchor the schema.
   - **Fix:** Insert one fully worked example after the force-couple section: pick a specific motion (e.g. arm abduction to 90°), name each force, draw vectors, walk through the couple step by step.

### Medium severity

4. **Missing signaling (medium)** — Section headings are flat ("4.3 Muscles", "4.4 Movements"). Reader can't tell what's foundational vs. derived. WM is spent on structure-finding rather than content.
   - **Fix:** Use signaling language: "*Foundational — read first:* the four rotator cuff muscles." "*Derived — return after Section 4.3:* common impingement patterns."

5. **Likely expertise reversal (medium)** — same prose density used for the introductory rotator cuff section and for the advanced GIRD section. Beginners drown; experts re-read material they already know.
   - **Fix:** Mark advanced subsections clearly; collapse expert-only material to summary form for the main flow with a "deep dive" pointer.

### Low severity

6. **Decorative imagery (low)** — page 8 has a stock photo of a tennis serve that adds no anatomical information. Mild extraneous load.
   - **Fix:** Remove, or replace with an annotated overlay of shoulder positions during the serve.

### What the chapter does well

- Vocabulary is pre-trained in section 4.1 before being used in 4.2+ (good intrinsic-load management).
- One table summarizes all rotator cuff muscle origins/insertions/innervations in one place — high element-interactivity material kept integrated, not split across pages.

### Top-3 changes ranked by ROI

1. Inline the figure-1 labels with their explanations (fixes split attention, single largest WM saving).
2. Insert one worked example for force couples.
3. Delete the redundant scapulohumeral-rhythm prose on pages 4 and 9.
