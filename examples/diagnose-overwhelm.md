# Example — DIAGNOSE: "this section feels overwhelming"

**User prompt:** *"I'm reading the Krebs cycle section in my biochem textbook and I just can't follow it. Why does it feel impossible?"*

**Skill output:**

---

**Dominant load type:** mostly **extraneous**, on top of genuinely high **intrinsic** load. Both are inflating WM use, but extraneous is the addressable one.

### Why it feels impossible (specific mechanisms)

1. **Intrinsic load is genuinely high, and that's not the textbook's fault.**
   The Krebs cycle has 8 steps, each with a substrate, a product, an enzyme, and a cofactor change (NAD+/NADH, FAD/FADH₂, GDP/GTP, CO₂ release). That's ~32 interacting elements. Element interactivity is the source of intrinsic load — you cannot understand step 4 without step 3's product in WM. No design choice can eliminate this; it has to be sequenced and built up gradually.

2. **The textbook layout is causing split attention (extraneous, fixable).**
   If the cycle diagram is on one page and the step-by-step explanation is on the facing page or chapters later, your WM is split between holding the diagram in mind and parsing the prose. That alone can blow past the ~4-element limit before any actual chemistry is processed.

3. **Probable redundancy (extraneous, fixable).**
   Many biochem chapters narrate the cycle in prose *and* show it as a diagram *and* list it as a table. For a learner who can read the diagram, the prose is duplicate information competing for the same WM slots.

4. **All steps presented at the same depth (extraneous, fixable).**
   If every step gets the same paragraph length, you can't tell that "step 1 (citrate synthase) is the regulated entry" matters more than "step 6 is just a hydration." Without signaling, your WM spends cycles deciding what to focus on.

### One concrete change to try first

**Stop trying to read the section linearly. Instead:**

1. Cover the prose with your hand. Look only at the diagram.
2. Trace the cycle once, naming only the substrate at each node. Don't worry about enzymes or cofactors yet.
3. When that's automatic (you can recite all 8 substrates), trace it again and add only **enzymes**.
4. Third pass: add cofactor changes (NADH/FADH₂/GTP/CO₂).

This is **isolated-elements sequencing**: process one dimension at a time before processing them simultaneously. Three light passes will load less than one heavy pass, even though you "read it three times."

If after that the prose still feels redundant — it probably is. Skip it and use it only as a reference when a specific step is unclear.

### What I am *not* claiming

- That the textbook author is bad at teaching. High-element-interactivity content is hard, full stop.
- That this is a "germane load" problem. Per de Jong's critique, claiming a learner needs more germane load is unfalsifiable. The actionable framing is: *your WM is overloaded; reduce extraneous sources, then chunk.*
